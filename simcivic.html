What Is SimCivic?

A Living,
Breathing World

Created by Truc Nguyen during her work with Fayette County Public Schools, SimCivic puts student teams in charge of fictional nations — managing 7 resources, negotiating formal trades, forging alliances, and building real physical cities to compete for the highest score.

Pilot Results — Stonewall Elementary, April 2026

33 fifth-graders participated (31/33 completed the survey). 93% said they'd do it again. Average enjoyment: 4.2 out of 5. 74% wanted an after-school SimCivic club.

Game Mechanics

How SimCivic Works

Six interconnected systems determine your fate. Master all of them — or find allies who cover your weaknesses.

01
Resources & Budget
Every round applies passive changes to your 7 core resources. Financial Planners must track everything precisely.
+3 Capital, +2 Happiness, +3 Raw Mats every round
−3 Food and −3 Water consumed every round
Correct budget = +5 Capital / Incorrect = −5 Capital
02
Diplomacy & Trade
Political rankings rise and fall through trades, alliances, and aid. Every transaction has diplomatic consequences.
+1% political ranking per trade occurrence
Ongoing alliances grant +2% per round
Breaking alliance: −10% political + both nations become ENEMIES
03
War System
Nations can declare war — but the costs are real. Winners gain from losers; all allies are affected.
Declaring war costs −10 Capital and −10% political
Winner gains +6 Food, Water, Raw Mats & Capital
Losing nation protected from war for 2 rounds; allies of winner get +2 all stats
04
City Building
Building physical structures earns score points. Judged on materials, explanation, location, and real-world purpose.
Support (roads, bridges, ports): 1–3 pts
Amenities & Necessities (parks, hospitals, schools): 3–8 pts
Creative structures with a purpose: 0–15 pts
05
Global Policy
Pass resolutions that reshape the rules of the game. Get 5 signatures from other nations' key roles.
5 signatures from other nations required to pass
Each passed policy = +3% political score
Coordinator can veto unbalanced policies
06
Aid System
Nations can request and receive aid at any time. Post-war nations get a special Aid Request system.
Any nation can request aid at any time
Declining ally aid causes greater relation damage
Relationship strength shapes aid impact

How It Works

A 5-Phase Journey

From classroom to competition, SimCivic follows a structured five-phase arc — each phase building on the last, creating an immersive real-world governance simulation that can run in a single day or across multiple sessions.

Prep
📋
Preparation
Optional skill-building phase where participants learn foundational concepts — civic governance, resource management, negotiation tactics, and presentation skills — to make the activity run smoothly.
Phase 01
🗺️
Nation Planning
Teams are assigned their nations, study their starting resources, and begin crafting strategies. Roles are distributed, and each team drafts an initial governance plan and sets the vision for their territory.
Phase 02
🤝
Rounds & Diplomacy
The heart of SimCivic. Nations enact policy cards, negotiate trades, form alliances, call global summits, and navigate real-time crises — all tracked live on the interactive scoreboard across multiple rounds.
Phase 03
🏗️
City Building
Nations sketch their city layouts on territory maps — considering deserts, bodies of water, and fertile land — then purchase building materials to construct 3D models of their infrastructure using popsicle sticks, tape, and pipe cleaners.
Phase 04
🎯
Presentation Prep
Nations compile their final case: documenting diplomatic decisions, resource management strategies, alliance choices, and proposed solutions to their territory's real-world challenges (flooding, pollution, food security, etc.).
Phase 05
🏆
Final Presentations
Teams present to a panel of judges — community members and industry experts. Judges award Innovation Points that can shift the final leaderboard. The highest-scoring nation is crowned the winner.
⏱️ Flexible timeline: Can run in a single 5–6 hour session or spread across multiple days. The pilot ran as an 8 AM–2 PM event at Stonewall Elementary — phases 2 and 3 naturally overlapped. Multi-session formats give students more time for thoughtful negotiation and decision-making.
Program Vision

Built for Every Classroom

SimCivic is designed for adaptability and scalable implementation across a wide range of educational settings — from elementary school through high school, from a single class period to a multi-day event.

🎓
Adaptable by Design
Resources, roles, rules, and territories can be adjusted to match any age group, grade level, and instructional goals — supporting foundational civic skills for younger students and advanced critical thinking for older ones.
📈
Scalable Impact
SimCivic can be delivered in classrooms, cafeterias, gyms, libraries, or community centers — and can integrate with after-school programs, the YMCA, Boys & Girls Club, or school-sponsored clubs.
🌐
Real-World Ready
Beyond academic content, SimCivic builds perseverance, communication, civic responsibility, ethical decision-making, and the ability to collaborate under high-pressure, real-time conditions.

What You'll Need

Technology & Setup

SimCivic is designed to run in a large open setting — cafeteria, gym, library, or community center — with a flexible tech setup that makes the simulation immersive, manageable, and engaging.

🖥️
2 Large Monitors
The first monitor serves as the host's comprehensive dashboard — manually enacting changes and maintaining a full activity overview. The second hosts a live shared negotiation sheet where nations schedule official trade meetings.
★ Large touch-screen monitors are usually already available at schools
📱
5 iPads (Ideal Setup)
One per nation — each iPad is the team's live dashboard to monitor resources, receive trade notifications, call global summits, enact policy and nation cards, and manage gameplay in real time from their territory.
★ The pilot ran successfully with 2 monitors alone. iPads elevate immersion.
💻
2–3 Chromebooks
One serves as the paid "research station" where nations spend Capital to look up real-world solutions to their territory's problems. Others serve as communication access points to the shared negotiation document.
Example: "How have cities fixed flooding?" · "Best negotiation tactics?" · "How to reduce pollution?"
🧱
Building Materials
Phase 3 transforms the room into a construction zone. Nations use popsicle sticks, tape, pipe cleaners, and similar craft materials to build 3D models of their cities on territory maps drawn on grid paper or blank canvas.
★ Each map incorporates geographic conditions — deserts, water bodies, terrain constraints
👥
Facilitation Team
Ideal setup: 1 game host + 5 nation facilitators (one per team). Each facilitator keeps their nation on task, encourages critical thinking, helps organize goals and mission, and bridges student communication with the host.
★ The game host can also serve as a facilitator, reducing staff to 4 + 1 host
🌐
Live Scoreboard
SimCivic's interactive web-based scoreboard tracks all 7 resources, live rankings, bilateral nation relations, alliance status, and global events in real time — accessible from any browser on any device via the shared domain.
★ Currently live at tr16cnguyen-dot.github.io/SimCivic/ · Built with Firebase real-time sync
